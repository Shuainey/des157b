Annotated Bibliography

Costanza-Chock, Sasha. Design Justice. MIT PRESS LTD, 2020.

Written by Sasha Costanza-Chock, an Associate Professor of Civic Media at MIT, this book is an exploration of how design might be led by marginalized communities, dismantle structural inequality, and advance collective liberation and ecological survival. It tackles the elephant in the room in that it addresses the various approaches and perspectives to the deisng process and depicts all of their advantages and pitfalls while also exposing the foul and harsh reality of the design realm, especially in the large corporations.

“City of Davis, CA.” Davis Centennial Seal, www.cityofdavis.org/city-hall/city-manager-s-office/arts-culture/davis-centennial-seal. Accessed 9 May 2023.

Designed by Susan Shelton, the Davis Centennial Seal is a representation of the histories behind the emergence of Davis and what it symbolizes. Crafted with expertice and polished with creativity, this Seal depicts the aspects of Davis city that may spark interest to the visitors who have not yet visted Davis before. From the origin of Davis, symbolic animals and crops, as well as the depiction of Univercity of California, Davis campus, the Seal can serve as a symbol for the city.

Vieira, Jon. “The Complete Guide to Cross-Cultural Design: Toptal®.” Toptal Design Blog, 14 Nov. 2019, www.toptal.com/designers/ux/guide-to-cross-cultural-design.

Cross-cultural design presents complex challenges, both linguistic and cultural. Despite this, most designers wrongly assume that designing products for different cultures simply requires language translation, switching currencies, and updating a few images to represent the local culture. Designers have to contend with different languages, dialects, and dimensions of national culture, as well as cultural differences in color psychology and mental models. Furthermore, reading direction from culture to culture adds another layer of complexity as text can be written left-to-right (LTR), right-to-left (RTL), and top-to-bottom. Cross-cultural designers must also consider everything from text to images, to navigation patterns and CTAs (calls-to-action) when designing for both LTR and RTL languages. Designers must use culturally appropriate imagery in products reaching across cultures as an image that may be perfectly acceptable in Western cultures may be considered inappropriate in some Middle Eastern countries. Finally, designers must also account for text expansion in different languages, which can cause text expansion of around 300%!
